Week 4: Policy and Earth Observation — Ahmedabad Heat Action Plan

Author

Shiyao Zhang

Published

February 10, 2026

Summary

Ahmedabad, Gujarat’s commercial capital with a population of roughly 8 million, experienced one of India’s deadliest heat events in May 2010 — an estimated 1,344 excess deaths over a two-week period as temperatures pushed past 46°C (Natural Resources Defense Council and Indian Institute of Public Health, Gandhinagar 2016). The city responded with the Ahmedabad Heat Action Plan (HAP), first published in 2013 and significantly revised in 2016, making it South Asia’s first formal municipal heat-health framework. The plan operates through four pillars: public awareness and community outreach, training healthcare workers to recognise heat illness, establishing a heat alert system using India Meteorological Department forecasts, and coordinating cooling centres and night shelters. These are genuine public health interventions and the plan has been cited as a global model for its speed and breadth.

The problem, and the reason it is interesting as a case study for this module, is that the 2016 plan contains almost no spatial analysis. It identifies heat as a citywide hazard but does not identify where within the city the burden is concentrated, which neighbourhoods have the least access to green space or shade, or which populations are most exposed. The plan’s interventions are broadcast-oriented — phone alerts, hospital training, public information — rather than spatially targeted. There is a brief mention of long-term data collection as a future objective, but no Earth observation dataset is named or used.

This is exactly the pattern the lecture described: policy frameworks exist at every level (the New Urban Agenda commits member states to reducing urban heat effects; SDG 11 targets safe, inclusive, green public spaces; the Sendai Framework covers disaster risk reduction), and municipal plans like Ahmedabad’s are nominally aligned with all of them. But the chain of alignment runs from global rhetoric to local action without a reproducible, spatially-explicit evidence base linking the two. The COP26 Beat the Heat handbook (United Nations Environment Programme 2021) was the first major international document to explicitly call for Earth observation integration into heat planning — which is a striking admission that until 2021, even the guidance documents were not making this connection.

Application

The gap in the Ahmedabad HAP is identifiable and addressable with readily available Earth observation data. The specific policy goal — reducing heat-related mortality — requires knowing where the thermal burden is highest, which groups are most exposed, and whether interventions are having measurable effect over time. Three EO datasets are directly relevant.

Landsat 8/9 Land Surface Temperature (LST) at 30 m resolution is the most established approach. The USGS Collection 2 Level-2 ST product provides atmospherically corrected surface temperature values directly, removing the resampling decisions that weakened the Fremantle Urban Forest Plan discussed in the lecture. Rather than using a single summer image — which risks capturing an atypical day — a seasonal composite over Ahmedabad’s April–June pre-monsoon window across multiple years (2015–2024) would produce a robust, time-comparable heat baseline. Areas of persistent high LST across years identify structural hotspots driven by impervious surfaces and vegetation deficit, not just weather variability.

Sentinel-2 NDVI provides the complementary vegetation layer at 10 m resolution. Green cover and LST are strongly negatively correlated in dense urban environments — the lecture cited estimates of up to 10°C temperature difference between vegetated and unvegetated adjacent neighbourhoods. Mapping NDVI deficits against ward-level boundaries in Ahmedabad would directly identify where tree-planting or green infrastructure investment would have the most thermal effect. This is operationally straightforward in Google Earth Engine and reproducible, in contrast to the one-off consultant-produced analyses that cities typically commission.

The equity question is where this becomes policy-relevant rather than just technically interesting. Li et al. (2022), examining 11 Texas cities, found a consistent spatial pattern: lower-income, historically disinvested neighbourhoods showed significantly higher LST values even after controlling for building density, because they had less canopy cover, more asphalt, and older building stock. There is no reason to assume Ahmedabad is different. Overlaying LST and NDVI composites with ward-level socioeconomic indicators — available from Indian census data — would allow the HAP to shift from a citywide alert system to a spatially-targeted intervention programme: prioritising cooling centres in high-LST, low-income wards; targeting tree-planting in NDVI-deficient areas with high population density; directing outreach workers to the highest-vulnerability blocks first.

One important caveat that the lecture raised: land surface temperature is what satellites measure, but ambient air temperature at head height is what humans actually experience. The connection between the two is real but not direct — reflective pavements can dramatically reduce LST while barely changing the air temperature a pedestrian feels. Any EO-based analysis feeding into the HAP should acknowledge this limitation and ideally be cross-validated against ground-level sensor networks or humidity-adjusted heat index data from weather stations, rather than presenting LST maps as straightforward evidence of human thermal comfort.

Reflection

What this exercise made clear to me is that the policy gap is not primarily a data gap. Landsat LST data for Ahmedabad is freely available, has been so since the 1970s, and requires no specialist hardware to access via GEE. The Ahmedabad HAP has been in operation for over a decade. The fact that these have not been connected is an institutional and capacity problem, not a technical one — which matches exactly what the lecturer described about Cape Town, where different government departments had data they were legally prevented from sharing with each other under normal timescales.

The lecture’s framing of the inequality–equity–justice spectrum is useful here. The current HAP operates at the level of equality: everyone in the city gets the same phone alert. An EO-informed approach would enable equity: directing more resources to the wards that are structurally hotter and socially more vulnerable. Genuine justice would mean addressing the underlying reasons those areas have less canopy cover and more asphalt — which is a land-use and investment history question that EO data can map but cannot solve.

I found myself genuinely uncertain about the direction of causality in the LST-deprivation correlation. Do poorer areas have higher LST because disinvestment means less tree planting? Or do higher LST areas become cheaper because they are less desirable to live in? Probably both mechanisms operate simultaneously, and the policy implication differs: one calls for targeted greening investment, the other calls for broader housing and land-use reform. An LST map alone cannot distinguish between them.

There is also a question I did not find a clear answer to: how would you actually get this EO analysis used? The lecturer’s honest account of trying to engage Fremantle’s planning department — being met with interest but no action, because they had already paid for an existing plan — suggests that the challenge is less about producing the evidence and more about timing. Getting satellite-derived heat maps into the room before a city publishes its action plan, not afterwards, is where the leverage is.
